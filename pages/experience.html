Ontological Closure and Structural Limits on Systemic AI

July 2026, International Conference on Machine Learning EIML Workshop

Collaborator: Poojak Patel, Illinois Math and Science Academy

Goodhart's Law (when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure) is typically treated as a problem of proxy degradation under optimisation pressure. We argue this framing conceals a deeper, prior structural condition: every evaluation framework is defined over a bounded ontological space, and Goodhart dynamics within that space are categorically unable to surface failures that lie outside it. We formalise this as Ontological Closure and show that it is the dominant failure mode for AI systems operating under unknown unknowns, distribution shift, adversarial novelty, evolving deployment contexts, and out-of-distribution inputs that the system and its evaluators have not yet imagined. We then construct a four-component framework for evaluation and governance adequate to open-ended deployment: failure space triangulation, consequential consistency testing, structurally adversarial evaluation institutions, and framework reflexivity. We ground the framework in two case studies (epistemic uncertainty in clinical AI and out-of-distribution perception in autonomous driving) and argue that in each, the dominant risk is not Goodharting within a well-chosen evaluation space, but the invisible exterior of the evaluation space itself.

Corruption Structure Determines Failure Mode in MRI Reconstruction

June 2026, Conference on Physics and AI 2026 at Stanford University

Collaborators: Poojak Patel, Illinois Math and Science Academy; Solomone Somani, Illinois Math and Science Academy

Standard evaluation of learning-based MRI reconstruction focuses almost entirely on random undersampling, leaving open how reconstruction fails under the broader range of physically realistic measurement perturbations that arise in clinical practice. We present a controlled evaluation framework grounded in k-space physics, where the structure of any measurement corruption directly shapes reconstruction failure. Applying three physically motivated k-space corruptions to knee MRI from the fastMRI dataset Zbontar et al. [2018], we show that corruption structure determines the qualitative character of failure independently of severity level. Random undersampling produces gradual degradation (normalized SSIM drop 0.134); structured line dropout produces catastrophic collapse with high cross-subject variance (normalized SSIM drop 0.877); and additive noise produces smooth, intermediate degradation (normalized SSIM drop 0.675). Notably, structured line dropout exhibits a high-variance regime at mild severity where visually similar corruption levels produce dramatically different failure outcomes across subjects, a non-obvious failure pattern absent from other corruption types. At severe severity, line dropout and additive noise both produce substantial degradation yet qualitatively distinct failure modes that aggregate PSNR evaluation cannot distinguish. These results argue for corruption-type-aware evaluation as a necessary component of reliable AI-based MRI reconstruction.

Prototyping New Systems for White Light Solar Flare Observation and Data Collection

June 2025 - August 2025, Astrophysics Group at SIUC

Mentors: Dr. Corinne Brevik, Southern Illinois University Carbondale; Christopher Mandrell, Southern Illinois University Carbondale

White light solar flares are violent reconnection events of the Sun's magnetic field that can emit a wide range of the wavelengths of visible light. These flares can be powerful enough to create significant infrastructural disruptions due to their geomagnetic effects and thus are a focus of heliophysical research. This study details the composition and analysis of three days of solar data on June 9th, June 10th, and June 20th. We also describe the development of a new data collection system through automation of the observatory, along with the problems faced in both data collection and development. We found no white light solar flares on the days we took data, but white light solar flares occurred on June 17th and June 19th, illustrating the need for more consistent data collection systems to reduce the chance of missing a flare event. The development of more automated data collection systems means we can increase the efficiency of our observations and possibly observe more white light solar flares in future studies.

Investigating the Changes in Flux Density of Methanol Masers in the Orion Nebula

June 2024 - May 2025, Earth and Space Sciences Group at WIU

Mentor: Dr. Esteban Araya, Western Illinois University;

Collaborators: Kenneth VanZuiden, Western Illinois University; Gabriel Sojka, Western Illinois University

The VLA Orion-A Large Survey (VOLS) collaboration used the Karl G. Jansky Very Large Array (VLA) telescope to observe a methanol maser in the Orion Nebula 20 times from April 3rd, 2022 to July 1st, 2022, corresponding to 20 different epochs of data. This study aimed to characterize this methanol maser in terms of flux density consistency, fast variability, and light wave polarization on both a short-term and long-term scale using these data, forming three objectives. The flux density was consistent for 17 epochs and inconsistent for 3 epochs based on the measurement errors, insinuating consistent calibration between pointings for most epochs. Within an epoch, the scans generally slightly differed in flux density over a 60-80 minute period, with mild evidence of fast variability in certain epochs. We saw no linear or circular polarization in any of the epochs. However, we saw many discrepancies in specific epochs such as offset pointing averages in epoch 2, an offset scan in epoch 3, negative sidelobes in a few epochs, and a software error in epoch 11. Through this study of multiple 2022 epochs, the maser’s behavior on a short time scale was analyzed more accurately than previous studies looking at epoch averages.
